\begin{abstract}
    A graph
    is 1-planar if it can be drawn in the plane such that each edge is crossed at most
    once---a natural generalization of planar graphs that has received increasing
    attention in graph drawing and beyond-planar graph theory.

    We search for the smallest non-1-planar graph.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Arguably among the most natural applications of a tool for recognizing 1-planar graphs is
finding the smallest non-1-planar instance in a family of graphs.
A particularly challenging variant of the problem is to
find the smallest 3-regular (cubic) graph that is not 1-planar. While it is easy to show
(see e.g., \cite{DKMW08}) that large random cubic graphs are not 1-planar, the
smallest such instance is unknown.

\newtheorem*{problem1b}{Problem 1}
\begin{problem1b}[\cite{11011110_cubic,mathse_cubic,oops}]
    What is the smallest cubic non-1-planar graph?
\end{problem1b}

In an attempt to answer the question, Eppstein~\cite{11011110_cubic} manually constructed 1-planar
drawings of several graphs and suggested that either the \texttt{Coxeter} graph or the \texttt{Tutte-Coxeter} graph
is not 1-planar. As pointed out in \cite{oops}, the former is indeed 1-planar.
The latter is conjectured to be non-1-planar in \cite{oops}.

Our goal is to find the \df{smallest sub-cubic non-1-planar} graphs. In the following, we
study properties of such instances. That is, a non-1-planar graph in which (i) the maximum vertex degree is $3$ and (ii) removing any vertex or any edge from the graph makes it 1-planar (or planar).

\begin{lemma}
    The smallest sub-cubic non-1-planar graph is biconnected.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    A 1-planar drawing of a graph can be composed from 1-planar drawings of its biconnected components. Hence, if all such components are 1-planar, then the
    graph itself is 1-planar too.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
    The smallest sub-cubic non-1-planar graph is cubic.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    Assume for a contradiction that the smallest sub-cubic graph contains a
    vertex of degree $2$; that is, $v \in V$ is connected to $u$ and $w$. Consider two
    cases:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item edge $(u, w) \notin E$: Then we can remove $v$ from the graph and
        insert a new edge $(u, w)$. The resulting graph $H$ is smaller and hence, is 1-planar. Take the drawing of $H$, remove the curve for $(u, w)$ and instead
        re-insert a path $(u, v, w)$; one of the two edges, $(u, v)$ or $(v, w)$,
        inherits (at most one) crossing of removed $(u, w)$, another one is crossing-free.

        \item edge $(u, w) \in E$ and there exists $x \in V$ such that
        $(u, x) \in E$ and $(w, x) \in E$: Then $x$ is a cut vertex (unless the graph is $K_4$), since it
        disconnects $u, w, v$ from the result of the graph. By previous lemma, we
        have a desired claim.

        \item edge $(u, w) \in E$ and $u$ and $w$ have no common neighbors:
        Then we can merge three vertices $u, v, w$ into a common one without creating
        parallel edges. Then draw the result and re-insert the three vertices back.
    \end{itemize}
\end{proof}
